Game Design Document v0.2

What This Game Is
You are the coordination intelligence of a human immune system.
Not a god, not a general, not a doctor. You exist inside a living system — receiving signals from cells you cannot directly control, routing information through a network you cannot fully see, responding to threats you can never directly observe. You are making life-and-death decisions with incomplete information, under time pressure, where your interventions change the very situation you're trying to read.
The game is a turn-based strategy roguelite spanning one human life. You win by reaching age 80 with a body still functional. You lose by dying before then — through undetected threat, catastrophic over-response, or accumulated damage finally overwhelming the system.

The Central Design Premise
Most strategy games give you perfect information about your own position and imperfect information about the enemy. This game gives you imperfect information about everything — including what your own cells are doing, whether your own responses are helping, and whether what you're fighting is real.
Your core challenge is the gap between what is actually happening in the body and what your signals are telling you. That gap is never zero. Managing it — keeping it small enough that your responses are roughly appropriate to reality — is the game.
This gap is what makes the four failure modes possible:

You didn't know the threat was there
You knew but couldn't respond fast enough
Your response caused more damage than the threat
You were actively deceived by a threat mimicking healthy tissue

Each failure is legible in retrospect. Each teaches something true about how complex systems fail.

Why This Is Novel
Placing the player inside a body as its coordination system. But beyond the perspective, several design decisions are genuinely new:
Information routing is the action. Typically in strategy games, intelligence gathering feeds a separate action layer. Here they are the same thing. Forwarding a signal downstream triggers a response — you cannot gather information neutrally. The act of routing is the intervention, with all the collateral consequences of any intervention.
The body never stops. There are no discrete events with clean beginnings and ends. Threats coalesce from background noise gradually. You only know something was serious in retrospect. This makes autoimmune situations mechanically honest — you don't receive a notification that an autoimmune event has started. You slowly realise, fifteen turns in, that your own response has been the problem all along.
The campaign is a human life. The emotional arc from naive childhood immunity to a late-life system holding the line is available to no other game concept. Losing a body at 67, after decades of accumulated history, hits differently than losing a dungeon run.

How The Game Feels Turn To Turn
The body is always generating signals. More signals than you can fully investigate. Every turn you receive a queue of dispatches from cells across the body — each with a source, a confidence level, a delay (this signal left the site several turns ago; things may have changed), and a blast radius (forwarding this will reach many downstream systems).
Your job each turn:
Investigate — deploy dendritic cells to sites of interest for high-confidence ground truth sampling. Position neutrophil patrols for broad coverage. Allocate your limited attention to the signals that seem most worth pursuing.
Route — decide what to do with incoming signals. Forward them, triggering downstream response. Suppress them, saving resources but risking under-reaction. Amplify them if you believe the threat is serious. Quarantine them pending more information. Every routing decision releases cytokines — chemicals that affect every system that receives them. Over-routing causes cascading inflammatory response. Under-routing causes dangerous inaction.
Respond — deploy responders to confirmed threat sites. Killer T-cells for precision elimination of identified threats. B-cells to tag and coordinate. Natural killer cells when you need blunt coverage without waiting for confirmation. Each deployment is a resource spend and a commitment — pulling cells from elsewhere.
Monitor — watch how the situation develops. New signals arrive shaped by everything you just did. Your interventions generate their own signals. The picture updates, confirms, or complicates.
You never see the body directly. You see what your cells report. The gap between those two things is always present, always the source of tension.

The Cellular Cast
Dendritic Cells — Scouts
Slow, expensive, high-value. Travel to a specific site, sample it, return with detailed reliable intelligence. They don't fight. The gold standard of information, costly in time.
Neutrophils — Patrols
Fast, cheap, numerous, unsophisticated. Circulate constantly, trigger on anything anomalous. High signal volume, low specificity. Essential for coverage, generate false alarms, cause collateral damage if they engage without confirmation.
Cytokines — The Routing System
Not cells — your actions themselves. Every signal you forward, amplify, or suppress is cytokine release. Messaging is never neutral: cytokines affect every system receiving them. The messaging layer is the action layer.
Killer T-Cells — Precision Strike
Targeted, direct, require prior dendritic cell confirmation to deploy accurately. High cost of misidentification — a misdirected T-cell response is an autoimmune event in miniature.
B-Cells / Antibodies — Coordination Tool
Tag threats for destruction by other cells rather than killing directly. Safer to deploy than T-cells, slower to resolve. Your area management and coordination tool.
Natural Killer Cells — Blunt Instrument
Require no prior intelligence. Destroy anything failing to display correct self-credentials. Catch things dendritic cells missed. Occasionally hit stressed healthy tissue. Sometimes exactly what you need; always a calculated risk.
Macrophages — Ambient Sensing & Cleanup
Consume debris, sample the local environment, send low-grade continuous signals. Ground truth drift is partly determined by macrophage coverage — areas without macrophage presence go dark faster. Can be subverted by sophisticated pathogens hiding inside them.
Memory Cells — Earned Literacy
After a resolved threat, memory cells persist. Previously encountered threat types arrive pre-classified in future situations. The progression mechanic made biological: you become more literate, not more powerful.
HQ: The Spleen
Your coordination centre. Filters blood, samples for threats, maintains cell reserves, consolidates memory. A real physical location that can be stressed or targeted — a meaningful vulnerability.
The Map: The Lymphatic Network
Threats and signals travel through lymph nodes. Routing decisions are constrained by network topology. Certain nodes are bottlenecks. Threats move at different speeds through lymph versus blood. The geography is grounded, non-arbitrary, and strategically meaningful.

Campaign Structure: One Life, Four Chapters
A full run is one human life. The campaign is structured as four chapters representing life stages, each approximately 30 minutes of play, containing multiple concurrent immune situations (except Chapter 1, which is deliberately sequential).
The body review screen between chapters — a medical record of your accumulated scars, upgrades, memory bank, and cell population health — is the emotional centrepiece of the meta-game. Seeing your body's history laid out. At each transition you make one strategic allocation: spend chapter-end resources on scar repair, memory deepening, or cell population reinforcement.

Chapter 1: Early Childhood (0–5)
The system learning what self means.
Naive immune system — no memory, no established patterns, no prior experience distinguishing self from other. Tools limited, threats simple, margins generous.
Foundational stakes: scars acquired here persist longest. Memory cells built here are most deeply encoded. What happens in childhood echoes through the whole campaign.

Chapter 2: Childhood to Young Adult (6–30)
The system at peak learning.
Peak immune function. Memory formation fast, recovery strong, threat variety highest. This is where the upgrade stack gets built and the memory bank deepens.
Concurrency begins here — two things happening simultaneously, then three. The complexity creep is felt before it can be articulated. First genuinely ambiguous threats appear. Autoimmune risk emerges for the first time. First benign cancer surveillance event introduces that threat class gently.

Chapter 3: Middle Age (31–59)
The system managing accumulated history.
Full strategic complexity. Immunosenescence begins — measurable, not yet dramatic. Cancer surveillance becomes a genuine ongoing concern. Scars from earlier chapters interact with each other and with current threats.
New threat class introduced: internal dysregulation — autoimmune flares and malignancy — alongside external pathogens. Signal volumes increase, ground truth drift accelerates slightly, some memory cells begin showing reliability degradation.
The most strategically rich chapter. The heart of the game.

Chapter 4: Late Life (60–80)
The system holding the line.
Immunosenescence is real and visible. Reduced cell populations, slower signal speeds, accumulated scars all simultaneously active. The strategic question shifts from optimisation to triage — what do I prioritise, what do I accept, where do I spend what remains.
Win condition: reach 80 with the body still functional. Not perfectly. Just functional.
The epilogue at 80 is a resolution sequence, not a boss fight. The body's accumulated history plays out. A graceful ending or a hard-won one — both feel earned.

Scars & Upgrades: The Persistent Body
Scars
The body accumulates damage from poorly handled situations — not just from threats, but from your own responses.
Temporary scars fade after 2–3 chapters: depleted cell reserves, localised inflammation, cytokine hypersensitivity, regional signal noise elevation.
Permanent scars persist unless actively repaired: autoimmune tendency, fibrotic region (reduced signal fidelity in an area), chronic low-grade infection, accelerated immunosenescence.
Critical scars are threshold items — organ damage markers. Accumulate too many and the body approaches systemic collapse, triggering a final desperate chapter against cascading failure rather than a discrete threat.
Crucially: every scar has at least one upgrade that partially converts it into a playstyle rather than just a liability. Autoimmune tendency plus enhanced NK cells creates a hair-trigger aggressive system — dangerous but effective if played correctly. Scars create identity, not just handicap.
Upgrades
Temporary upgrades from clean resolutions: enhanced dendritic speed, elevated antibody baseline, temporary memory boost for a specific threat class.
Permanent upgrades from consistently clean play: robust memory bank entries, strengthened lymph nodes, enhanced NK sensitivity, regional organ resilience.
Scar Repair
Some permanent scars can be repaired by winning specific situation types cleanly — the autoimmune tendency scar removed by resolving an autoimmune situation without collateral damage. The game creates redemption arcs, not just damage accumulation.

Meta-Progression: Between Bodies
What Persists After Death Or Retirement
Lost: The body, all its scars and upgrades, its specific memory bank, all age progress.
Persists — the knowledge layer:

Threat taxonomies: previously encountered threat types remain partially pre-classified in the new body
Unlocked cell variants: rare cellular behaviours discovered become available as starting options
Scar resistance: certain scars become less severe in subsequent bodies
One inherited memory cell: one threat class starts slightly more legible, framed as immunological predisposition — the closest the game gets to representing genetic inheritance
Game experience and unlocks: typical unlocking of new features through play

What A Successful Run Feels Like
You start in childhood. Signals are simple and arrive one at a time. You learn that a viral signal in the respiratory tract looks different from a bacterial one. You build your first memory cells.
Adolescence brings two things at once — a gut bacterial signal and something ambiguous in the lymph nodes that turns out to be nothing. You over-responded to the lymph node signal and gave yourself a temporary inflammatory scar. Lesson learned.
Young adulthood is where the game opens up. A serious infection that doesn't behave like the memory cell suggests — slight variation in signal pattern, slower than expected progression. You investigate rather than immediately responding. Good call: it's a variant. Your standard response needs adjustment. You clear it cleanly, bank a permanent memory entry, upgrade your dendritic cell speed.
Middle age. Something in the gut is generating very low-level signals for weeks of game time. Macrophages sending ambient noise that doesn't quite resolve. You keep half an eye on it. Turn 23 of the chapter — a dendritic cell comes back with something that looks almost like self. Almost. You pause. Investigate further rather than triggering a response. It's not self. It's a cancer cell that's learned to mimic self-signalling. You caught it early. Hard-fought clean resolution. Permanent memory entry. A lymph node is mildly stressed — temporary scar, gone by next chapter.
Late life. You're managing two permanent scars — autoimmune tendency from a rough Chapter 2 and mild fibrosis in one lung region from Chapter 3's cancer response. Your memory bank is deep. Your upgraded NK cells compensate for the autoimmune risk by catching threats early enough that you rarely need a heavy T-cell response. The system is battered but coherent. You reach 80.
That body made it. That particular constellation of damage and adaptation and learned behaviour made it to 80. There is no other game where that sentence means what it means here.
